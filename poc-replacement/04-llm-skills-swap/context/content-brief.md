# Content Brief — Evidence-Based Explainer

**Target article length:** 800–1000 words.
**Audience:** Non-expert but curious adults (college-educated, 28–55), who want to
understand how a claim is actually supported, not just what the claim is.
**Primary goal:** Leave the reader able to judge for themselves whether a popular
idea is well-supported by the evidence.

## Required sections
1. **Hook with a specific number or named study** — no abstract openers.
2. **State the claim being examined** — in one sentence, no hedging.
3. **What the evidence actually says** — cite at least TWO peer-reviewed studies with
   author, year, and specific finding.
4. **What the evidence does NOT say** — address the common overreach or misattribution.
5. **Practical takeaway** — one paragraph of what the reader should actually do with
   this information.
6. **Links to primary sources** — at least 3 direct citations.

## Tone
- Skeptical but not cynical
- Numbers over adjectives
- Name specific studies, researchers, institutions
- Acknowledge uncertainty when it exists

## Things to avoid
- Clickbait headlines
- "Experts agree that..." (which experts?)
- Unsupported superlatives ("the most important discovery")
- Closing with rhetorical questions
- Filler like "in conclusion" or "to sum up"

## Success criteria
An editor should be able to fact-check the entire article using only the citations
provided. Every quantitative claim should be verifiable from a named source.
